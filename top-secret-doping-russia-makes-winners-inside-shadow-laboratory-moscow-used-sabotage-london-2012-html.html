Pictures inside Russian lab which helped cheat London 2012 doping tests

These are the first pictures from inside the 'shadow laboratory' used by Russia to cheat the world of sport by sabotaging samples of athletes using performance enhancing drugs.

Packed with modern testing equipment, the lab was used to pervert the results of major competitions in a state-sponsored programme of cheating, it is claimed.

It was named in a World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) report as being involved in a cover-up to destroy athletes' suspicious urine samples.

Two labs were at the heart of Russia's 'calculated state-sponsored doping programme', the report claimed. 

The shadow lab and the official WADA-accredited Russian anti-doping lab, both in Moscow.

The allegation in the report is that the shadow lab screened and destroyed suspicious athletes' samples before sending clean ones to the WADA lab for official testing.

Run by Georgian forensic toxicology expert Dr Giorgi Bezhanishvili, the shadow lab's existence was disclosed by an independent commission (IC) set up by WADA, to investigate ‘serious doping allegations’ in the wake of the 2014 German TV documentary, ‘Top Secret Doping: How Russia makes its winners’.

The documentary accused Russian sport’s governing body, the ARAF, of 'state-sponsored doping'. 

WADA employed former president, Canadian lawyer Dick Pound to chair the report. He was assisted by experienced legal counsel Richard McLaren and Gunter Younger, head the Cybercrime Department in the Bavarian State Police.

The report said: 'There is sufficient corroborated evidence to conclude that the second laboratory was assisting in the cover-up of positive doping results by way of the destruction of samples.' 

It went on to say the shadow facility was 'used as a first step to identify test samples of Russian athletes who have suspicious or positive urine samples'.

It stated that pre-screened samples that were not positive were then sent to the official anti-doping WADA lab.

HOW RUSSIA 'SABOTAGED' LONDON 2012 OLYMPICS 

The damning 323 page WADA report said the London 2012 Olympics was 'sabotaged' by athletes who 'should not have not been competing'.

It said the ARAF [All Russia Athletic Federation] was wrongly informed of 23 Russian athletes subject to investigations of abnormal drugs test results.

The report stated the 'early warning' gave the ARAF 'an advantage over other countries' because it was able to identify the athletes and 'wrongfully' delay their ADRVs [Anti-Doping Rule Violations], applications, allowing them to compete at the London 2012.

It said six athletes who competed at the Games had 'unexplained and highly suspicious delayed notifications' which allowed them to compete 'despite their abnormal ABPs' [Athlete Biological Passport] - and two won medals.

It added evidence of conspiracy and cover up among ARAF's coaching staff indicated 'widespread and institutional abuse'.

Head of the official lab Dr Grigory Rodchenkov resigned in disgrace earlier this month after he admitted destroying 1,417 suspicious samples - hours after his laboratory was suspended over accusations it was part of a state-sponsored doping programme.

The IC said the second lab, run by six staff, may not have been as effective as the WADA accredited one at screening samples, which could explain why some samples sent to Rodchenkov were destroyed.  

Evidence on the role of the second lab was given to the IC on a confidential basis due to fear of reprisals against the witnesses, the report said. 

Known as the 'Laboratory of the Moscow Committee of Sport for Identification for Prohibited Substances in Athlete Samples', it is is controlled by the Moscow city government rather than federal authorities.

Unlike the WADA lab, it continues to function. 

The second lab was originally located at the 78,000-seater Luzhniki Stadium in Moscow, host of the 2018 FIFA World Cup Final.

It is now located in glass-fronted building close to the Red Army Theatre, headquarters of the Moscow Centre for Sports Innovative Technologies.

It was founded in 2008 as part of a 'Fair Sport' programme designed by the Moscow Committee of Physical Culture and Sport.

Speaking in 2011, 'chief specialist' Ruslan Braitsev explained how the two laboratories operated. He said: 'We analyse substances from WADA's prohibited list - these are anabolic steroids, diuretic corticosteroids, stimulants and drugs'.

A television report on it at the time said 'the most up-to-date equipment is used to analyse samples'.

Dr Bezhanishvili, 58, has not commented on the report.

While the report failed to establish a formal link between Rodchenkov's former laboratory and the shadow one, it said that he and Dr Bezhanishvili knew each other.

Yet there were close informal links, the report said.

When the shadow lab was set up, its staff were sent on internship to the WADA-accredited Russian anti-doping laboratory, a senior sports official has said.  

Head of the Innovative Sports Technologies Centre David Chichua explained the link between the shadow lab and the WADA-accredited on.

He said: 'The laboratory will take the samples of our sportsmen, students of Moscow sports schools, and test them.

'In case positive samples are found, they will be double checked: the so called sample B will go to the Russian Anti-Doping Centre [run by Rodchenkov].'

Action would be taken only if a positive result was confirmed by the WADA laboratory, it was said.

There is no suggestion Mr Chichua was involved in any deception in the report.

In its damning 323-page report, WADA said the London 2012 Olympics had been 'sabotaged' by athletes who 'should not have not been competing'.

It said there had been a cover-up and delay by ARAF in investigating athletes with abnormal passport profiles who should have been prevented from competing at London 2012. 

It recommended lifetime bans for four Russian coaches and five athletes.

It said London 2012 800m gold medalist Mariya Savinova-Farnosova should be banned. She admitted, in secret video recordings, to using the banned steroid oxandrolone.

Two labs: Report said the 'shadow lab' had the same testing capabilities as the official lab run by Rodchenkov

Russian Ekaterina Poistogova, London 2012 bronze medalist should also be banned, it recommended, adding: 'Ms Poistogova was identified in the ARD [German TV station] secret video recordings admitting to taking a number of prohibited substances.'

Controversially, the the report said that some Russian athletes feared for their personal safety if they did not subscribe to the internal workings of the national team.

One said when they were considering speaking out about team practices they were warned by another athlete, 'leave it, otherwise you might accidentally get in a car accident'.
