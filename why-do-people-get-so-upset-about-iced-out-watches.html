Why Do People Get So Upset About Iced-Out Watches?

There is a video of Cardi B and her iced-out Patek Philippe Nautilus that I know by heart. “A hundred thou on my wrist, bitch,” she declares, waving her sparkling piece at the camera. “Our land might depreciate, our schmoney might depreciate, but this right here? I could sell it anywhere, anytime, and open like 10 salons, bitch. I’m so proud of myself.”

Her watch, in other words, is an investment: a tangible object that will not only hold value, but actually appreciate. Sounds about right … right? When I first found the video, I didn’t bother to question the validity of her statement. As a newly minted enthusiast, it didn’t cross my mind to fact-check that this $100K watch would indeed still be $100K (or more) in five, 10, 20 years. I assumed that all watches, blinged out or not, would appreciate in value.

The vitriol alone lets you know it’s about more than just a watch.

It’s been more than a year since I first saw the Cardi video, and my horological knowledge has, in fact, appreciated. Studying watches has led to an expansion of my vocabulary, and now I understand the meaning of words like factory and aftermarket. Factory is a term used to indicate that everything on a watch comes from its manufacturer—it remains untouched by any third party. Aftermarket, conversely, refers to anything added by customers or dealers after the watch has left the factory, from a funky-colored dial to a bustdown bezel. To some collectors and experts like Kelly Yoch, the director of client relations at Watches of Switzerland, the latter term might as well be a slur.

“Adding aftermarket customization of any kind devalues the watch from its original form in which the craftsmen, who are truly artists, envisioned,” she explains. A watch’s price instantly declines the second you pop an aftermarket diamond on it. Equipped with this information, I revisited Cardi’s video, asking myself whether or not she’d make a profit if she ever had to sell her Nautilus. The simplest answer to that question would be no.

Shareif Ziyadat//Getty Images

Cardi B

But, as with most things in life, the world of watches is anything but simple. Yoch, for example, represents just one facet of the community. In her words, she’s a purist, existing in a camp with other collectors who believe that factory is king and anything that deviates is lesser than. I respect Yoch and understand that her steadfast principles stem from a place of experience and true authority, but I’ve also noticed that within the purist community, there’s a different type of enthusiast seething with opinions that deserve closer scrutiny.

Anytime I repost a photo of a customized watch on my watch account, Dimepiece, the DMs reliably come in from followers signifying their distaste with the puke emoji or its verbal equivalent. It started to irk me how comfortable people were with doling out their criticism, more often than not fired at watches belonging to Black celebrities. Facts are facts, and personalizing one’s watch does, in most cases, devalue it, but these attacks were brimming with a sinister energy.

Why is everyone so pressed? Do they really care that much about ‘integrity’?

“The vitriol alone lets you know it’s about more than just a watch,” private jeweler and GIA-certified Gemologist Zenobia Morrow explains. “In Cardi’s case specifically, there are layers of misogyny, racism, and classism at play.” Here, Morrow begins to illuminate what had been gnawing at me instinctively. Why is everyone so pressed? Do they really care that much about “integrity”?

Isabella Proia, specialist with Phillips Watches, echoes Morrow’s sentiment from within the rarefied air of the auction world. “Whether it’s overt or subconscious, these negative comments come from a place of institutional racism,” she states. “I’ve been in the industry long enough to know that aftermarket diamonds are not the sole purview of any one race or ethnicity, but the comments on these images are often overly vicious and derogatory.” Of course, it’s not only Black people who wear customized watches, but it’s difficult to ignore the consistency with which they are targeted.

The term bustdown refers to an original luxury watch being taken apart (a.k.a. bust down), and then drilled and filled with diamonds or other gemstones. “This kind of customization is informed by the music,” Morrow explains. “Sampling and remixing are foundational elements of hip-hop music and culture, so it makes sense that the remix concept would extend to style, jewelry, and, of course, watches.” Indeed, this approach came onto the scene in the ’90s through hip-hop, with artists like Master P commissioning jewelers to personalize Rolex President watches. At the time, this watch was the crème de la crème for the rap game, and adding a little extra shine took it to a whole new level.

Fast-forward to the early 2000s’ Bling Era, when ice was thrust into the mainstream via the increasingly popular, New Orleans–based Cash Money Records crew. “Medallion iced up, Rolex bezelled up / And my pinky ring is platinum plus.” Rappers like Lil Wayne had every high school jock and their mother talking about “bling bling,” and in the meantime, aftermarket diamond settings only got more sophisticated.

L. Cohen//Getty Images

Ludacris and his watch.

New York–based jeweler Greg Yüna is an example of someone who does it right, with clients including Russell Westbrook, who rocked a Patek Philippe Nautilus, expertly after-set with pavé diamonds and baguette-cut sapphires, on the red carpet of the Met Gala. Yes, another Nautilus—while Audemars Piguet, Richard Mille, and, of course, Rolex watches are frequently customized, Patek Philippe watches are currently the ultimate watch to bust down.

Of course, this may feel like a slap in the face to those who deem Patek Philippe the epitome of haute horology. What a violation it is to devalue a watch that, in the eyes of many collectors, is perfect. Pure.

Pure is a loaded word, weighed down by historical baggage.

Pure is a loaded word, weighed down by historical baggage and indicative of rampant elitism within the luxury industry. “Any customisation, as far as [Swiss watchmakers are] concerned, is an act of vandalism,” Alfred Tong wrote for British GQ in 2019. “Patek Philippe watches are conceived of as heirlooms to be handed down over the generations. Its famous advertising slogan ‘You never actually own a Patek. You merely look after it for the next generation’ shows us that conservatism runs deep within this company.” Good for you if you get passed down a Patek. And even if you did have the money to afford one of the brand’s highly sought-after references (which will set you back $100K minimum), good luck finding one.

Mike Coppola//Getty Images

Russell Westbrook and his Greg Yüna original.

Virgil Abloh considered the concept of purity, using the binary of “the tourist” and “the purist” as the organizing principle with which he creates. “A tourist is someone who’s eager to learn, who wants to see the Eiffel Tower when they come to Paris,” he said in 2018. “The purist is the person who knows everything about everything.”

Shortly after Abloh’s untimely passing, HODINKEE’s Nick Marino identified this approach as the driving force behind the designer’s mind-boggling takes on not one, but two, Patek Philippe Nautiluses. “His personal piece was a blackened [reference] 5726, ionically pulverized and ruthlessly murdered out by the wizards at MAD Paris,” Marino writes. “Rarely does one watch say so much. It said: I paid for this, and I can do what I want with it. It said: Your finished product is my starting place. It said: I can top your masterpiece.” Behind this bold statement is an underlying current of openness and curiosity rarely found within a purist.

Still, there are ways to think about vintage watches that don’t buy into the murky ideology around purity. Writer and anti-racism consultant Danielle Prescod says that for her, the appeal of an original watch is twofold: It’s the aesthetic, but it’s also about being mindful of your impact on the world. Having relocated from New York City to New Orleans, Prescod lives in the hometown of bling, yet when she went to buy her first luxury watch, she gravitated toward a vintage, diamond-free 26mm Rolex Datejust. In her Dimepiece interview, Prescod rhapsodized about the joys of hunting down vintage, which, she argued, is a more sustainable approach. “I would encourage more people to explore vintage, explore consignment. Go greener, get comfortable with that,” she told me. “There’s still this stigma that, if you have money, you should buy things brand spanking new.”

Prescod pointed out that the watch conversation tends to leave out Black audiences entirely unless it’s about customization. The notion that Black watch fans care only about bling is “a general racist idea that gets solidified when people are shown a narrow representation of what they think a certain group of people does,” Prescod explains. “There are going to be examples of Black people with ‘traditional’ watches, as well as examples of non-Black people with so-called unrefined watches. And that is why a full spectrum of representation is always important.” It’s incumbent on watch media and other horological spaces to make sure they’re telling the full story.

Ultimately, watch wearing all comes down to personal taste. “You should be buying a watch because you enjoy it,” Greg Yüna says. “The way I look at it is this: If a bustdown watch is what you like, we can do it, and we can do it correctly. But if you are looking at these pieces as investments, keeping everything original is always the best move to make.”

So let’s ask the question again: If Cardi B were to sell her Nautilus, would she make back that “hundred thou” on her wrist? Morrow puts it best: “Hello? This is Cardi B. Any discrepancy around aftermarket customization is easily negated by the provenance of it being her watch. She is an international superstar, pop culture phenomenon, and a bona fide fashion icon. Ninety million records sold and she’s just getting started.” Tell that to the purists.
