PD James leaves 22m in her will with most going to her two daughters

Talent: Novelist PD James, who died last year

Bestselling crime novelist P. D. James left more than £22million in her will when she died last year aged 94, it has been revealed.

As well as producing 20 highly successful books, Phyllis James – later Baroness James of Holland Park – had a shrewd eye for property investment and owned two houses, four flats and a beach hut, probate records show.

James, who combined writing with a career as a senior civil servant, had ten of her 14 novels featuring detective Adam Dalgliesh turned into ITV dramas between 1983 and 1998, with Roy Marsden playing the lead.

The BBC took over the series in 2003, casting Martin Shaw as the Scotland Yard commander in dramatisations of two more books, Death In Holy Orders and The Murder Room.

The writer, who died last November, left the bulk of her net fortune of £22,403,597 before inheritance tax to her two daughters. It is believed that her estate will have to pay more than £8 million in tax.

James also left £90,000 to each of her five grandchildren and her secretary Joyce McLennan, £20,000 to her sister Monica and a total of £83,000 to seven others, including two of her domestic helpers. She left further legacies totalling £85,000 to five Oxford and Cambridge university colleges, despite never studying for a degree herself because her father did not approve of higher education for girls.

The writer, who was an Anglican, left one of her flats in Oxford to the restoration fund of St Mary Magdalen church in the city. She gave £30,000 to the Royal Society of Literature, as well as £10,000 to the Prayer Book Society for which she was a lay patron.

Her will specified that her main home in Holland Park, West London, and a second flat in Oxford should be left to her daughter, Clare Flook of Cambridge.

Her other daughter, Jane McLeod of Oxford, receives her flat next to Tower Bridge in Central London, as well as her cottage and beach hut in Southwold, Suffolk.

The author’s will, drawn up in July 2014, left another flat in West London to her grandson James McLeod. But she removed this legacy two months later, stating she had ‘provided for the gift during my lifetime’. The author left her manuscripts to Cambridge University’s Girton College, while her daughters received everything else – including her literary estate.

Drama: From left, Wendy Hiller, Fiona Fullerton and Roy Marsden in ITV series A Taste For Death

James, who became known as the new Queen of Crime after the death of Agatha Christie in 1976, was born in Oxford before moving to Ludlow, Shropshire, and then attending secondary school in Cambridge.

She had a difficult childhood with unhappily married parents and an aloof father, who worked as a middle-ranking Inland Revenue official.

Her mother was admitted to a mental hospital when she was 14, leaving her to look after her younger brother and sister. James joined the Inland Revenue after leaving school – which she described as ‘the worst possible choice’ of career.

She resigned after 18 months and went to work at the Festival Theatre in Cambridge, where she met Anglo-Irish medical student Connor Bantry White. The couple married in 1941.

Her husband served with the Royal Army Medical Corps during the Second World War, but suffered a breakdown. He spent most of the rest of his life on psychiatric wards and died in 1964 after mixing alcohol and drugs.

James worked from 1949 as an administrator in the NHS, eventually moving to the Home Office where she ran the criminal justice department.

Her first novel, Cover Her Face, was published in 1962. As well as Dalgliesh, James also created the popular character Cordelia Gray, a detective who features in two of her books.
