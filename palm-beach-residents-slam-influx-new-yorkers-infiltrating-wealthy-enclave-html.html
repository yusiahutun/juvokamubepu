Palm Beach residents slam influx New Yorkers infiltrating their wealthy enclave

Wealthy Palm Beach residents have slammed 'rude' New Yorkers for infiltrating their city and taking up their parking spots, filling up their schools, and even hogging their tennis courts. 

As cost of living skyrockets in Big Apple - which ties with Singapore as the most expensive city in the world - New Yorkers have been fleeing the bright lights of Manhattan for the tax-free, lower crime rate state of Florida.  

New Yorkers, such as Ivanka Trump and Jared Kushner who daily runs in the sun, and Jon Bon Jovi, have snapped up more properties in Florida, long-term residents are beginning to become irritated by their presence, especially in Palm Beach. 

'People here go about their business quietly, go out and go home early. Now there is so much rudeness,' Shannon Donnelly, a society columnist for the Palm Beach Daily News told the New York Post. 'There are contretemps over parking spaces and waitresses are crying. 

'One man’s vibrant is another man’s noisy and unruly.'

Real-estate broker Francie Leidy said the big city-goers are even driving like they're in the streets of Manhattan, filling the quiet Florida town with car horns and aggressive driving. 

'It took me 30 minutes just to cross the bridge — and what’s with all the horn honking?' she told the Post. 

Yana Schlesinger, whose husband owns the Brazilian Court hotel, is also finding the newbies presence encroaching on her daily activities. 

'I can’t get on a tennis court here anymore,' she told the Post.  

However, Schlesinger isn't totally upset with the Northerners marching into town. 

'They are bringing life and energy to Palm Beach,' she said. 

But it's not just the streets of Palm Beach and and the top tier tennis courts that that the newcomers are encroaching upon, according to residents, but the private schools. 

'Friends can’t even get their kids into a [private] school,' Leidy told the Post. 'But we can all say thank you for the bump up to our real estate values!' 

'People here go about their business quietly, go out and go home early. Now there is so much rudeness,' Shannon Donnelly said. Another resident, Yana Schlesinger, said: 'I can’t get on a tennis court here anymore' 

Palm Beach residents also say they notice New Yorkers a mile away as they don't shop at the same places, nor have the same old money style as them 

Donnelly agreed, saying: 'The guy who bought the most expensive place in Palm Beach comes down with his kids. There is no room in the schools but he comes up with a gigantic donation. Suddenly they have room.' 

Despite the nuances, the long-term residents' social clubs - such as the Everglades - are proving to stay more exclusive to the real Palm Beach residents. Some even predict the New York hedge fund wives might want to ditch the city after finding out they have a hard time joining the clubs, a source told the Post. 

However, New Yorkers have brought their own culture along, with New York restaurants - such as, La Goulue and Le Bilboquet - opening up shop in the coastal city. 

President and CEO of the Palm Beach Chamber of Commerce, Doug Evans, said the newcomers are less invested in what Palm Beach has to offer right at home and more interested in travel. 

'[A new resident] I was with yesterday just came back from taking his kids to Antarctica,' Evans said. 'It used to be that the social thing here was the galas and parties, but now all the packed restaurants are just as exciting and you can’t get into them unless you book six months in advance or know someone.' 

In addition, Palm Beach residents say they are quick to pick out a New Yorker from any crowd, as their style differs from old money residents who don pearls and dress in florals.

New Yorkers are wearing more monochromatic looks and cashmere scarves and the signature all-black attire. 

'It’s the worst you can imagine — we see jeans with holes in them,' long-term resident Nancy Traylor told the Post.

Donnelly agreed, said she saw 'hedge fund guys' wearing Gucci belts at the post office and driving away in Maybach and Bentleys. 

'I didn’t even have to look to know the license plates were from New York,' she said. 'Big cars and good jewelry should stay home during the day.' 

Some are even calling it a 'new era' and don't believe newcomers, like Jon Bon Jovi - who recently swiped up a $43million property there - are going to be caught dead in loafers anytime soon.

'The guys who can afford houses here now want the beauty, safety and sophistication, but don’t need to buy into the old establishment,' financial adviser Brian Hogan told the Post. 

Within the influx in New Yorkers coming to Palm Beach, property developers are spending loads to build more commercial and residential buildings, as the Big Apple suffers from high vacancy rates. 

Stephen Ross, 82, whose net worth is around $12billion, has said that people in the Northeast are looking for warmer climates a lot earlier than retirement. 

'People are... relocating for jobs, not retirement, and companies are looking [for office space],' Ross, a chairman of property developer Related Companies, told Bloomberg. 

'It's tax issues, and there's the security issues. There's just the ease of living [in the South].' 

Since the pandemic, and with the increasing popularity of remote work, cities like New York have been hurting, as increasingly empty office buildings leave billions of dollars' worth of vacant space. 

In the past two years, major tech, finance, and law firms have ditched big cities like New York and Chicago for the comfort of the tax-free state. 

Citadel, a hedge-fund company, recently left Chicago for Miami. Apollo Global Management and Blackstone Inc., both originally based out of New York, have also relocated to Florida, according to Bloomberg.
