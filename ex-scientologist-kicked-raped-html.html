Ex Scientologist 'was kicked out after she was raped'

A former member of the Church of Scientology's elite Sea Org has told how the Church shamed, punished and ultimately kicked her out after she was drugged and raped as an 18-year-old.

Most painful of all, in her darkest hour, Siberian-born Katrina Reyes who signed the Sea Org's infamous billion-year-contract at 11 years old, was betrayed by the one person she thought she could turn to: her mother.

The Sea Org's rules concerning sexual contact effectively turned Katrina's rape into a crime against Scientology and converted her from victim to perpetrator.

Speaking exclusively to DailyMailTV Katrina recalled the moment her mother told her she could be kicked out: 'My first thought was, "Well if they kick me out aren't you coming with me?"

'And she said, "No. I have too much here."

Katrina Reyes, 30, tells DailyMailTV how she was kicked out the Church of Scientology after she reported she had been raped 

Katrina signed her billion-year contract when she joined the Sea Org when she was just 11 years old after she and her mother moved to Florida from Siberia 

Today 30-year-old Katrina is happily married to software engineer, Jose. She lives in Austin, Texas with him, their two dogs and son Nolan who will turn two in May. Life is good. Life is 'normal,' she says. 

It is more than ten years since Katrina's mother sat with her traumatized daughter and told her she would not go with her if she were forced to quit the only life she'd ever known, but Katrina's devastation remains sharp to this day.

It has taken her years and 'hundreds of hours of therapy' to process what happened to her when, as an 11-year-old girl living with her mother in Russia she was co-opted into the Sea Org on the promise of a better life in America.

Katrina's mother Yelena was just 17 when she had her. Looking back, Katrina admitted: 'She was a young mother who didn't have her priorities straight - men was a number one priority and going out partying.'

Katrina's grandmother had been introduced to Scientology when she visited Florida for work. The older woman was immediately hooked, signing up as a member of the Sea Org - the church's quasi-military administrative body - after barely two weeks in the country.

According to Katrina her grandmother thought life at Scientology's Flag headquarters in Clearwater might help her own daughter straighten her life out.

She recalled: 'My grandmother painted a picture of a good life, a purpose, a job, helping people, you never have to worry about a roof over your head or me having an education. I guess that was very appealing - American dream for my mother.'

She and her mother were recruited into the Estates Project Force (EPF) - a Sea Org boot camp of sorts designed as a crash course to being qualified to be given a post, or job

Katrina claims her mother Yelena (pictured with her husband) chose to stay in the church after she was kicked out and the two have been estranged since 2014 

Yelena (right) had Katrina (left) when she was only 17 years old and was a young mother who didn't have her priorities straight - men was a number one priority and going out partying,' she says 

She said: 'We signed our billion year contracts. I remember to this day. I read 'a billion years' and I thought that was maybe just a way of saying, 'I completely and utterly dedicate my life.'

'At the same time I remember thinking: this is what my mother is doing so I have no choice. Wherever she goes I just have to follow her.'

And there was little about her life in Eastern Europe that Katrina thought she would miss. Katrina, real name Yekaterina Tihonova, spent most of her childhood in Barnaul, Siberia. It was, she said, 'like a third world country.

'We lived in a wooden house, no heating, no plumbing, no electricity. I'd have to haul the water from buckets, it was a pretty miserable existence.'

Her grandmother 'coached' her to tell officials that she had every intention of returning to Russia so that she and her mother would be granted visas.

She said: 'I remember arriving at Tampa airports and everything was nice and clean and different. I remember the big automatic glass doors. That was the first time I'd ever seen them. I was like, "Wow! Everything is so civilized here."'

The excitement was short lived. She and her mother were taken to live in Sea Org housing - The Hacienda Gardens, rows of dilapidated and overcrowded buildings.

They shared a one-bedroom apartment with a dozen other women (men and women are housed separately).

She said: 'The living room had three bunk beds in it, then there was one dining room and three more bunk beds in the bedroom. The apartment was so old. It was filthy, disgusting, there were cockroaches - absolutely huge things - mildew was everywhere because of the humidity, tiles coming off the wall.'

She shared a bunk with her mother as the two were recruited into the so-called Estates Project Force (EPF) - a Sea Org boot camp of sorts designed as a crash course to being qualified to be given a post, or job.

She and her mother were taken to live in Sea Org housing - The Hacienda Gardens, rows of dilapidated and overcrowded buildings

The Hacienda Gardens, where Katrina lived and worked, and is heavily fortified with a motion sensored fence, security and a manned gate

Katrina said: 'The only thing I was excited about was that my mother was going to be away from her old life, things she used to do [I thought] she's finally going to concentrate on me instead of men, partying and abandoning me.'

'I thought it was an opportunity for us to have a clean start.'

It was not to be. Days consisted of early starts, often heavy manual labor and hours of study. Katrina questioned everything, unable to go along with the theories espoused.

Meanwhile her mother absorbed it all and passed out of the EPF in three months. She was given a job and Katrina was informed that her mother would be moving into different housing while she stayed on behind.

She said: 'That was very upsetting for me when I found out she can't live with me. We joined September 26, 1999. By New Years she was already done. I never lived with her again.'

After a year Katrina was assigned the post of Tech Page which meant she organized the roster of Scientologists who came to Clearwater to be audited. Sessions could cost up to $1,000 an hour during which members were grilled and their answers read on L Ron Hubbard's E-meter - a primitive polygraph test.

It was a huge money making enterprise, Katrina recalled. The priority was to get as many members in and through the process as possible, then sign them up for more courses, more auditing, more spending.

Katrina said: 'The first briefing of the day, the leader David Miscavige's questions were always, "Who blew? (who ran away?) Lawsuits, Media and then how much money Flag made." 

Katrina was declared a 'suppressive' person by the church in 2015

The two worked long hours as Sea Org members and Katrina was only allowed to see her mother for 40 minutes a day 

Katrina enjoyed the contact with a world outside the Sea Org's rigid constraints but it made her yearn for freedom of her own. She admitted: 'I'd see these people with their nice cars, nice house, cell phones, dressed nicely, good children…it made me envious. I just wanted freedom, to get in a car and go anywhere I want.

'We were herded in buses every day. At The Hacienda we had a perimeter fence, and when I once touched it, three security guards on bikes immediately surrounded me asking, "Who the f*** touched the fence?" Then I realized it was motion activated.'

Days were long - starting at 6am and sometimes not finishing until two, three, four in the morning. Failure to hit quotas for sales led to physical and mental punishment - sleep deprivation, being forced to eat rice and beans for breakfast, lunch and dinner. The only glimmer of light in her day came at 7pm when Katrina ate her dinner and had a chance to spend 40 minutes with her mother.

Celebrities would often spend time in town, staying at one of the church-owned hotels such as the Osceola Inn or the Sandcastle, where Katrina's mother worked.

Katrina recalled: 'You weren't allowed to go near Tom Cruise. You weren't allowed to speak or look at him. But we knew he was coming to Flag, as we'd have to white glove everything. It was a huge operation.

'We had to clean all the waiting areas, auditing rooms, then it would be inspected by someone who puts on a white cotton glove and touches every surface.'

Cruise would stay in the penthouse at the Osceola Inn, she said. He moved everywhere with a 'huge entourage' and his presence was always heralded by the arrival of two large black Cadillac SUVs.

John Travolta was a bit more relaxed. She said, 'He was lovely. I would pass by him in the hallways and restaurants and he would look you in the eye and say, 'Hello, how are you?'

Kirstie Allie used to stay at the Sandcastle and Katrina recalled her mother telling her that they had to refurbish all her rooms and provide her with 'orange Egyptian Cotton sheets.'

She stayed in touch with her mother long enough for the older woman to come to her wedding in 2013

Katrina now lives with her husband Jose in Austin, Texas. In 2016 when she was five months pregnant with her son, she went to Clearwater to try to visit her mother, but her attempt was ignored

She said: 'She was always dressed like she'd been to the gym - wearing sweats, hair tied back, unbrushed, but she was a hot mess!'

'When you're a celebrity in Scientology,' Katrina said, 'there's nothing that can't be done for you.'

But the gulf between the celebrity experience and that of the Sea Org members was huge.

One thing that sticks in Katrina's throat to this day happened back in 2004 when Tom Cruise celebrated his birthday on Scientology's cruise ship the Freewinds.

Sea Org members earned just $50 a week, minus tax, for hours of grueling work. Katrina said: 'I remember it was Friday when we picked up our pay and you would get your little envelope with cash in it.

'Somebody from the office that looks after VIPs was at the door going, "We need a donation for Tom Cruise's birthday. It's mandatory - $35."

'So out of the $50 you got paid, minus tax, two or three weeks in a row they were taking $35 as a donation to pitch in for Tom Cruise's birthday present which we found out was like a really expensive dirt bike.'

It was a miserable existence that Katrina knew deep down was not for her, but she couldn't see a way out. In the end her departure from the Sea Org came in the most devastating circumstances.

In 2006 Katrina managed to persuade Sea Org officials to allow her to travel to Russia to get her citizenship passport - a legal requirement. On the final night of her trip, staying with relatives in Siberia, she argued with a family member so violently that she felt she couldn't stay there that night.

She called some old neighborhood friends and they went to a bar to drink and 'chill.' 

Katrina says none of the members were allowed to 'go near or look at' Tom Cruise. She also revealed a time when she was forced to donate $35 of $50 weekly earnings to chip in for the actor's birthday present 

She also met another celebrity Scientologist John Travolta who she described as 'a bit more relaxed'

To this day Katrina does not know exactly what happened but she believes a guy she did not know, who bought her a beer, had laced it with the so-called 'date rape' drug, Rohypnol.

She said: 'After [I drank it] I kind of blacked out. I remember walking up some steps in a weird apartment building, I remember little bits and pieces.

'The next think I remember waking up in bed with two guys next to me and realizing I'd been raped and I was still a virgin - in Scientology you're not allowed to have any sexual relations until you're legally married.

'Now I look back on this and realize how bad this Scientology brainwashes you to the point where my first thought wasn't, "Oh my God I need to call the police, I need to go to the hospital." 

'My first thought was, "I'm going to get into trouble with the church because I've broken one of the core rules."

Katrina was due to return to Florida that night so she gathered her clothing from the floor, washed herself off in the bathroom and left, flying back to the States as planned. She told no-one.

She kept her silence for a week, putting on a brave face and terrified that somebody would 'see through her.' But then the fear that she might be pregnant or might have caught an STD trumped her fear of being punished for her 'misdemeanor.'

She said: 'I remember thinking, they have to be sympathetic.'

She was wrong. Katrina had no choice but to tell a man - all the security guards were male.

She said: 'I explained to him what happened to him and he wanted it in great detail.'

Katrina returned to Clearwater, Florida - the headquarters of Scientology - in 2008 but she was still a Scientologoist  and not 'in good standings' due to her departure from the Sea Org

The next thing she knew she was locked - literally - in a security room with a guard outside the door, or nearby 24/7.

To her horror she was isolated from the other girls and instructed to sit and write down all her 'overts' and 'withholds' - indiscretions committed openly and those that have been kept hidden.

She explained: 'The theory is that if something bad happened to you it's because you have done something bad in the past and you're pulling this in.'

After about two weeks she was allowed to see her mother. She said: 'We sat on the stoop where she lived and we had this conversation about what happened. I just remember my mother being so stone cold. There was just no human reaction to it.'

Over the next few days Katrina was subjected to a series of 'Security Checks' - interrogations on the E-meter.

She said: 'It was done by a male, they were all males. They told me they didn't believe me. They told me I was making it up just so I could leave the Sea Org.

'They asked me the same account over and over focusing in on details.'

Today Katrina believes it was a blessing of sorts that she could not remember what happened to her and so didn't have to relive the trauma under questioning.

The questioned focused in relentlessly on sex. Katrina recalled: 'He was asking me: "Have you ever had heavy petting or gone too far with a boyfriend?

'The next question was, "Have you ever been physically or sexually assaulted in the past?"

'And I have. My stepfather physically and mentally and sexually assaulted me.'

Katrina told her interrogator about a time when her stepfather had told her to strip naked and beaten her so badly with his belt that he left welts on her skin. She was eight years old.

She said: 'So I'm going through this with this man and the next question that came out of his mouth was, "Did you enjoy it? Did you misbehave on purpose so he would hit you this way?" 

'And I just lost it. I said, "F*** you. I'm done. I'm not going to answer any more questions because this is actual torture right now."

An hour or two later she was informed she was being kicked out of the Sea Org and sent back to Russia.

By then Katrina had a green card but she was still so browbeaten by Scientology she didn't think to fight it as she was taken to the airport and watched as she got on the plane. 

A security guard accompanied her as well as her mother, whom she had asked for, to make sure Katrina got on the plane and, she believes, her mother didn't.

She said, 'I was really mad at my mother then. Once again you're abandoning me.'

With no experience of life outside the Sea Org, few resources and little education, Katrina scratched by over the next couple of years working where she could until she had saved enough to return to America.

Russia, after all, was a foreign country to her. In 2008 she came back. She was still a Scientologist, and not 'in good standings' due to her departure from the Sea Org. 

As hard as it is to understand she wanted to regain her place in the church because it was all that she knew and it was her link to her mother.

But just a couple of days after her return to Clearwater, Florida two Sea Org members bumped into her in a bank. 

The next thing she knew a church security guard angrily approached her and said, 'You interbulated (a Scientology term meaning to bring up bad feelings) those Sea Org members. Get the f*** out of Clearwater or I'm going to declare your a**.'

To be 'declared' a suppressive person was the thing Katrina feared most because it meant disconnecting from her mother and grandmother. 

Scientologists are not allowed to communicate with 'suppressive' people and though the church denies the policy of disconnecting exists, Katrina's own experience says otherwise.

Katrina headed to Orlando. She worked and she began dating the man who would become her husband. She stayed in touch with her mother long enough for the older woman to come to her wedding in 2013 but the relationship was strained almost to breaking point even then.

She claims the church harassed and called her constantly trying to get her to sign up for 'services' to get her back in good standings'. But distance and her husband's influence started to loosen the ties that once bound her.

Eventually, when her own mother called telling her to stop communicating with known 'suppressive' people on social media something in Katrina snapped.

She said, 'Not even my husband has the right to tell me who I can and can't befriends with. I live in the United States. I live in a free country. Nobody's going to control me. I'm done having this conversation.'

Katrina hasn't spoken to her mother since November 2014.

In 2016 when she was five months pregnant with her son, she went to Clearwater to try to see her.

She said, 'She was crossing the street and I called out her name, then I called out, "Mom!"

'She turned and looked at me. She definitely spotted I was pregnant. She saw me and then she turned around and ran away.'

Yet, even after everything that has happened to Katrina, she has remarkable compassion for her mother. She believes that she is 'trapped in a mental prison.'

And should she ever break free Katrina will be there to start their relationship afresh.

She said, 'One of the last conversations I had with her, I told her I would never change my number and even ten, 20, or 30 years down the road you can knock on my door at three o'clock in the morning and I will open it to you.'
