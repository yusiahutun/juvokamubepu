The little girl who became the iconic face of the last catastrophic tornado to hit Moore, Oklahoma, 14-years ago has revealed her little sister is unaccounted for following Monday's tragedy in the town.

Many remember Aleah Crago, 14, as the 'Mud Baby', rescued after being found face down on the ground following the May 3rd, 1999 tornado that ripped through the town killing 44 people.

Speaking to CBS' Inside Edition, Crago expressed concern that her little sister Marisa, 13, who still lives in storm ravaged Moore is fine, saying, 'I don't know if she's safe.'

All those years ago on a typical spring day the weather suddenly took a turn for the worse and a storm of historic magnitude formed over the Oklahoma plains.

The huge F5 category tornado, which was classified as 'incredible,' caused $1.1billion in damage and the strongest winds ever recorded on Earth were registered as the massive twister barreled through at 318 mph.

During the terrific storm of 1999, Aleah Crago, only 11 months old, was ripped from her helpless mother's arms by one of the series of twisters that struck.

Her mother, Amy Crago was rushed to hospital, but not before she begged Deputy Robert Jolley to find her missing baby.

Aleah Crago became known as the 'Mud Baby' when Deputy Robert Jolley discovered her face down covered in mud after she flew out of her mother's arms during the 1999 Moore, Oklahoma tornado

Hours passed, but suddenly, a nurse walked by Amy and said according to News9.com, 'Are you the one missing a 11-month-old baby?'

The nurse revealed what Oklahomans have come to call a miracle. The entire episode was captured on dashcam video camera as Deputy Jolley finds the baby face down, covered in mud not far from where she left her mother's arms.

'I just started removing and removing dirt from her eyes, then she cried,' said the then 27-year-old Jolley to the Herald Tribune in 1999.

'I never thought I would be so happy to hear a baby screaming.'

Debris: The 1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak that struck Moore, Oklahoma was a vigorous severe weather event that lasted from May 3 through 6 of that year

A tornado near Anadarko, Oklahoma, on May 3, 1999 - from the series of storms that raged through the area - killing 141 people

Because of her state when found, Aleah Crago has always been known as the 'Mud Baby' to whoever saw the footage.

Despite having no memory of the disaster which she miraculously avoided, Inside Edition asked how she survived the 1999 tornado and Crago had this to say: 'It was luck and God wanting me to be here on this Earth today.'

The 1999 Moore tornado prompted the National Weather Service to send out the first-ever Tornado Emergency, a severe weather message used only in extreme cases where a massive tornado is about to impact a densely-populated area.

Devastation: An aerial view of an entire neighborhood destroyed by Monday's tornado in Moore, Oklahoma

A tornado emergency was issued on Monday before the two mile wide tornado struck the town of Moore.

Because of the staggering wind speed recorded during the 1999 tornado Bridge Creek–Moore tornado, there was speculation that the Fujita Scale of measurement would be modified to include a F6 catergory for winds that exceeded 318 mph.

However, it was decided that the damage caused by an EF5 tornado leaves little room for any higher or more severe category.

The day after the outbreak, then President Bill Clinton issued a federal disaster declaration for Oklahoma and 1,600 people left homeless overnight of May 3 were put into emergency Red Cross shelters.
